    The most common fuel used in boilers is natural gas, followed by heating oil and occasionally liquid petroleum gas. Less common are boilers which burn coal pellets or biomass, which usually comes in the form of wood chips). Electric central heating boilers are also available.

    New boilers require a 90% efficiency or higher (an A or B energy efficiency rating), which is often accomplished through condensing technology. If your boiler is more than 15 years old, it’s unlikely they reach these benchmarks.

    The main plus of a combi boiler is that they provide instant hot water while keeping you warm. On top of that, they’re more subtle - the absence of a hot water tank makes for a more slight appliance. There is a trade off though - the flow is slower than if it was coming from a cylinder, so for those of you who love a bath, you had better be prepared to wait a bit longer for it to run. Modern boilers generally no longer have tanks in the loft to 'pressurise' the system through gravity. Instead they are sealed systems, and typically only require manual topping up from the mains water supply when the internal pressure has dropped (usually because of tiny leaks). This is a simple operation that takes a few seconds, and involves opening a valve on a pipe below the boiler.
    ...and radiators?

    Despite their name, radiators do not just give off radiant heat, they actually deliver most of their heat
    through convection;

    air warmed by the radiator naturally rises while cool air falls relative to it. This results in the warmed air
    circulating, warming and the 'space' in the room.

    The pipework may also be connected to a hot water tank, which in turn provides a supply of hot water.
